Averaging rotations (i.e., quaternions) may not be as simple as you expected. Based on the result of this work, this post goes into the details why a simple weighted average of quaternions does not work and how to implement the real interpolation between quaternions. Part of this post is inspired from Quaternion Weighted Average. Thank Daniel.

The Wrong Way: Simple Weighted Average of Quaternions

Problem Definition

Before I go into the details of today’s topic, let me first give a relatively formal definition to the problem I will discuss in the later sections.

(Weighted Average of Rotations) Given a set of rotations (quaternions) q1,q2,⋯ ,qnq_1, q_2, \cdots, q_nq1​,q2​,⋯,qn​ and their associated weights α1,α2,⋯ ,αn\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \cdots, \alpha_nα1​,α2​,⋯,αn​ where ∑iαi=1\sum_i\alpha_i=1∑i​αi​=1 and αi≥0\alpha_i\ge 0αi​≥0, we would like to calculate the weighted average rotation q~=f(q1,⋯ ,qn,α1,⋯ ,αn)\tilde{q}=f(q_1, \cdots, q_n, \alpha_1, \cdots, \alpha_n)q~​=f(q1​,⋯,qn​,α1​,⋯,αn​) such that
(1) q~\tilde{q}q~​ is continuous at each qiq_iqi​ with fixed (α1,⋯ ,αn)(\alpha_1, \cdots, \alpha_n)(α1​,⋯,αn​);
(2) q~\tilde{q}q~​ is continuous at each αi\alpha_iαi​ with fixed (q1,⋯ ,qn)(q_1,\cdots,q_n)(q1​,⋯,qn​).

This definition points out for a desired weighted average of rotations, we would like it to maintain robust to slight changes of any of the sub-rotations as well as to slight changes of weights. As we will see soon, however, most popular solutions do not adhere to these two properties, and thus creating the so-called jump rotation phenomenon.

Wrong Way #1: N-lerp as Weighted Average

N-lerp is short for Normalized Interpolation. It’s a widely-used, simple quaterion interpolation strategy to blend multiple quaternions. Basically, an N-lerped quaternion q~nl\tilde{q}_\text{nl}q~​nl​ can be calculated as:

q~nl=∑i=1nαiqi∥∑i=1nαiqi∥\tilde{q}_\text{nl} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n\alpha_iq_i}{\Vert \sum_{i=1}^n\alpha_iq_i\Vert} q~​nl​=∥∑i=1n​αi​qi​∥∑i=1n​αi​qi​​

The norm at the denominator is necessary to make sure q~nl\tilde{q}_\text{nl}q~​nl​ is a unit quaternion, thus representing a rotation.

To see what’s wrong with the simple N-lerp method, let’s look at simple case.

Assume we are interpolating two quaternions, one is a fixed identity quaternion, i.e., qid=(1,0,0,0)q_\text{id}=(1, 0, 0, 0)qid​=(1,0,0,0). The second is a quaternion qqq simply rotating around the z\mathbf{z}z axis, i.e., the world up direction. But in the following examples, the rotation axis of qqq will slightly deviate from the world up direction z=(0,0,1)\mathbf{z}=(0, 0, 1)z=(0,0,1) so as to let you see how the rotation axis and angle will change as rotation changes. We also set α1=α2=1/2\alpha_1=\alpha_2=1/2α1​=α2​=1/2.

As a result, the final interpolated quaternion will be:

q~nl=12qid+12q∥12qid+12q∥\tilde{q}_\text{nl} = \frac{\frac{1}{2}q_\text{id}+\frac{1}{2}q}{\Vert\frac{1}{2}q_\text{id}+\frac{1}{2}q\Vert} q~​nl​=∥21​qid​+21​q∥21​qid​+21​q​

Merging both, only the top half space of the plane will be covered by q~nl\tilde{q}_\text{nl}q~​nl​. The bottom half space, will never have the chance to be reached. This is where the jump rotation comes from: the jump change of the rotation axis changes the direction the quaternion is interpolated, and thus changes the final interpolated quaternion.

You may ask, why does the rotation axis have to be flipped? Can you just maintain the rotation axis and let the rotation angle span [0,2π)[0, 2\pi)[0,2π)? Good question! But it will still cause jump rotation at where angle 000 or 2π2\pi2π, in which case the interpolated quaternion will only span the right half space of the plane. The same problem again.

In fact, we can take a closer look at the the we compute q~nl\tilde{q}_\text{nl}q~​nl​. Let’s try to derive a closed form of it.

The final quaternion q~nl\tilde{q}_\text{nl}q~​nl​ spans over half of the rotation range of qqq, meaning that it either spans over [0,π)[0, \pi)[0,π) if θ∈[0,2π)\theta\in [0, 2\pi)θ∈[0,2π), or spans [−π/2,π/2)[-\pi/2, \pi/2)[−π/2,π/2) if θ∈[−π,π)\theta\in [-\pi, \pi)θ∈[−π,π). This is exactly what we can expect from what we’ve discussed above.

This can be easily extended to multiple quaternions. More quaternions there are, more prone to slight changes the average quaternion will be.

Wrong Way #2: S-lerp as Weighted Average

If you are familiar with spherical interpolation (S-lerp), you may wonder if it’s possible to maintain continuity using S-lerp. Unfortunately, the jump rotation issue will still arise even if you use S-lerp.

Let’s use the same example of N-lerp. The first quaternion is qid=(1,0,0,0)q_\text{id}=(1, 0, 0, 0)qid​=(1,0,0,0), and assume the second quaternion as q=(cos⁡(θ/2),sin⁡(θ/2)n)q=(\cos(\theta/2), \sin(\theta/2)\mathbf{n})q=(cos(θ/2),sin(θ/2)n). The weights are 1/21/21/2 for both qidq_\text{id}qid​ and qqq. By the definition of S-lerp (I use the vanilla S-lerp definition for simplicity), we have the S-lerped quaternion q~sl\tilde{q}_\text{sl}q~​sl​:

q~sl=(qqid∗)12qid=q12=(cos⁡(θ/4),sin⁡(θ/4)n)=q~nl\begin{aligned} \tilde{q}_\text{sl} &= (qq_\text{id}^*)^\frac{1}{2}q_\text{id}\\ &= q^\frac{1}{2}\\ &= (\cos(\theta/4), \sin(\theta/4)\mathbf{n})\\ &= \tilde{q}_\text{nl} \end{aligned} q~​sl​​=(qqid∗​)21​qid​=q21​=(cos(θ/4),sin(θ/4)n)=q~​nl​​

This result is equivalent to N-lerp, which is not surprising as S-lerp is still manipulating raw quaternions.

We can use the following figure to illustrate why N-lerp and S-lerp are not working in our trivial case:

The angle of the result quaternion q~nl\tilde{q}_\text{nl}q~​nl​ is a periodic function with respect to the rotation angle of qqq. Though we stipulate that angle θ\thetaθ is within [0,2π)[0, 2\pi)[0,2π), we can actually extend it to R\mathbb{R}R by adding a period of 2π2\pi2π. But in the game engine, the value will be reduced into [0,2π)[0, 2\pi)[0,2π). It’s clear that at the point of x=2kπx=2k\pix=2kπ, the function is discontinuous, and this therefore causes the jump rotation issue.

The Right Way: Matrix Decomposition for the Largest Eigen-value

The jump rotation problem is rooted in the double cover property of quaternions, i.e., qqq and −q-q−q represent the same rotation, so that quaternions provide a 2:1 mapping of the rotation group. Ideally, changing the sign of qqq should not change the result average, but as we’ve observed above, it indeed changes the result, which is not what we would want.

We would like a way, serving as a 1:1 mapping to the rotation group, to help us disambiguate from the double cover issue. Matrix, does serve this purpose!

Special Orthogonal Matrix Is 1:1 Mapping to Rotation

First according to Wikipedia, we call the subgroup of orthogonal matrices with determinant +1 the special orthogonal group, denoted by SO(3)\textrm{SO}(3)SO(3). Then we can easily see that every rotation can be represented uniquely by a matrix M∈SO(3)M\in\textrm{SO}(3)M∈SO(3). This can be proved by showing that any rotation is a linear transformation of the standard basis x,y,z\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{z}x,y,z, which can be represented by a matrix in SO(3)\textrm{SO}(3)SO(3). For the inverse, any matrix M∈SO(3)M\in\textrm{SO}(3)M∈SO(3) forms a unique orthonormal basis, which represents a unique rotation upon the standard basis.

Next, we prove that the group of quaternions is 2:1 mapping to SO(3)\textrm{SO}(3)SO(3). This is obvious because the matrix presentation of a quaternion q=(x,y,z,w)q=(x, y, z, w)q=(x,y,z,w) is:

Q=[1−2y2−2z22xy−2zw2xz+2yw2xy+2zw1−2x2−2z22yz−2xw2xz−2yw2yz+2xw1−2x2−2y2]∈SO(3)Q=\begin{bmatrix} 1-2y^2-2z^2 & 2xy-2zw & 2xz+2yw\\ 2xy+2zw & 1-2x^2-2z^2 & 2yz-2xw\\ 2xz-2yw & 2yz+2xw & 1-2x^2-2y^2 \end{bmatrix} \in\textrm{SO}(3) Q=⎣⎢⎡​1−2y2−2z22xy+2zw2xz−2yw​2xy−2zw1−2x2−2z22yz+2xw​2xz+2yw2yz−2xw1−2x2−2y2​⎦⎥⎤​∈SO(3)

This is a rotation around vector (x,y,z)(x,y,z)(x,y,z) by a angle of 2θ2\theta2θ with cos⁡(θ)=w\cos(\theta)=wcos(θ)=w. It’s clear that both qqq and −q-q−q are mapped to QQQ.

We can understand quaternions as the process of rotation, whereas conceive matrices as the result of rotation, represented as the basis after rotation.

Problem Reformulation

Right now, the problem becomes: given a set of rotation matrices and their associated weights, how do we compute the average rotation?

A trivial idea is that, now that each matrix represents a basis in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3, we can interpolate these sets of basis. I will revisit this idea later, but now we can leverage a simpler idea to calculate the average rotation. It’s based on a particular definition of average: the average rotation should minimize a weighted sum of the squared Frobenius norms of the rotation matrices, which can be formulated as follows:

q~=arg⁡min⁡q∑i=1nαi∥Q(q)−Q(qi)∥F2\tilde{q}=\arg\min_{q}\sum_{i=1}^n\alpha_i\Vert Q(q)-Q(q_i)\Vert^2_F q~​=argqmin​i=1∑n​αi​∥Q(q)−Q(qi​)∥F2​

Based on this definition, we can derive a very neat closed form of q~\tilde{q}q~​, which maintains a nice property of continuity of interest. I call this method, M-lerp, short for Matrix Interpolation for rotations, and call the final quaternion as q~ml\tilde{q}_\text{ml}q~​ml​.

In this follow subsection, I will go into the details of deriving q~ml\tilde{q}_\text{ml}q~​ml​ step by step, basically following this work, but with more elaborations on some of the missing steps.

Theorem 3: Quaternion Continuity of M-lerp

Without loss of generality, we view q~ml\tilde{q}_\text{ml}q~​ml​ as a function of q1q_1q1​, i.e., q~(q1)=arg⁡max⁡qq⊤Mq\tilde{q}(q_1)=\arg\max_{q} q^\top Mqq~​(q1​)=argmaxq​q⊤Mq where M=∑i=1nαiqiqi⊤M=\sum_{i=1}^n\alpha_iq_iq_i^\topM=∑i=1n​αi​qi​qi⊤​. We will show that this function is continuous on q1q_1q1​.

For ease of illustration, we rewrite MMM as M=αqq⊤+∑i=1nαiqiqi⊤M=\alpha qq^\top+\sum_{i=1}^n\alpha_i q_iq_i^\topM=αqq⊤+∑i=1n​αi​qi​qi⊤​ where all variables are fixed except qqq. Then, the average quaternion will be q~=arg⁡max⁡q′q′⊤Mq′\tilde{q}=\arg\max_{q'}q'^\top Mq'q~​=argmaxq′​q′⊤Mq′.

We first show that the mapping from a real symmetric matrix MMM to its largest eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ is continuous.

Lemma 3.1: The mapping from a real symmetric matrix MMM to its largest eigenvalue λmax(M)\lambda_\text{max}(M)λmax​(M) is continuous.

We begin with that fact that the spectral norm of MMM is given by its largest singular value, which is the square root of the largest eigenvalue of MM⊤MM^\topMM⊤. Since MMM is symmetric, we have MM⊤=M2MM^\top=M^2MM⊤=M2, and its largest eigenvalue is the squared largest eigenvalue of MMM, and thus the spectral norm of MMM is MMM’s largest eigenvalue.

On the other hand, we know that the spectral norm map is continuous, and thus the map M↦∥M∥2=λmax(M)M\mapsto \Vert M\Vert_2=\lambda_\text{max}(M)M↦∥M∥2​=λmax​(M) is continuous.

Then, we prove the following lemma.

Lemma 3.2: The mapping from a unit vector qqq to matrix M=qq⊤M=qq^\topM=qq⊤ is continuous regarding the Frobenius norm.

Assume q0q_0q0​ is any unit vector and M0=q0q0⊤M_0=q_0q_0^\topM0​=q0​q0⊤​. For ∀ϵ>0\forall \epsilon >0∀ϵ>0 and qqq close enough to q0q_0q0​ (in terms of the angle between qqq and q0q_0q0​):

∥M−M0∥F=Tr((M−M0)⊤(M−M0))=Tr(M⊤M−M⊤M0−M0⊤M+M0⊤M0)=2−2(q⊤q0)2=2−2cos⁡2θ=2sin⁡θ\begin{aligned} \left\Vert M-M_0 \right\Vert_F &= \sqrt{\text{Tr}\left((M-M_0)^\top(M-M_0)\right)}\\ &= \sqrt{\text{Tr}\left(M^\top M-M^\top M_0-M_0^\top M+ M_0^\top M_0\right)}\\ &= \sqrt{2-2(q^\top q_0)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{2-2\cos^2\theta}\\ &= \sqrt{2}\sin\theta \end{aligned} ∥M−M0​∥F​​=Tr((M−M0​)⊤(M−M0​))​=Tr(M⊤M−M⊤M0​−M0⊤​M+M0⊤​M0​)​=2−2(q⊤q0​)2​=2−2cos2θ​=2​sinθ​

Lemma 3.3: The mapping from a unit vector qqq to the eigenvector of the largest eigenvalue of M=qq⊤M=qq^\topM=qq⊤ is continuous. That is, q↦umaxq\mapsto u_\text{max}q↦umax​ is continuous, where Mumax=λmaxumaxMu_\text{max}=\lambda_\text{max}u_\text{max}Mumax​=λmax​umax​.
