Which Splines Do You Need? A Comprehensive Dive into Common Splines

Posted on 2023-05-03 Edited on 2023-07-23 In 数学 - 图形学 Disqus: Word count in article: 14k Reading time ≈ 13 mins.

This post introduces some widely used splines in computer games including Bézier curves, cubic Hermite splines, Catmull-Rom splines, B-splines, Kochanek–Bartels splines, etc. We show these splines can be bridged through re-formulation. Then, we focus on the extension and generalization of Bézier curves and introduce mathematical methods to create dynamic smooth splines. Last, we develop a simple Editor in Unreal Engine to enable easy creation of splines under different constraints and conditions.

Common Splines

Bézier Curve

The most typical and widely-used spline might be Bézier curves. Bézier curve leverages a set of control points to define a smooth and continuous curve generated by interpolation between these control points. Using the De Casteljau’s algorithm,[1] we can easily express a Bézier curve in terms of a sum of Bernstein polynomials:

B(t)=∑i=0npibi,n(t), bi,n(t)=(ni)(1−t)n−iti\mathbf{B}(t)=\sum_{i=0}^n\mathbf{p}_ib_{i,n}(t), ~b_{i,n}(t)=\binom{n}{i}(1-t)^{n-i}t^i B(t)=i=0∑n​pi​bi,n​(t), bi,n​(t)=(in​)(1−t)n−iti

When there are four control points, the generated curve is called cubic Bézier curve. A cubic Bézier curve can be expanded as:

B(t)=(1−t)3p0+3(1−t)2tp1+3(1−t)t2p2+t3p3, 0≤t≤1\mathbf{B}(t)=(1-t)^3\mathbf{p}_0+3(1-t)^2t\mathbf{p}_1+3(1-t)t^2\mathbf{p}_2+t^3\mathbf{p}_3,~0\le t\le 1 B(t)=(1−t)3p0​+3(1−t)2tp1​+3(1−t)t2p2​+t3p3​, 0≤t≤1

The derivative of an nnn-th order curve is:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mathbf{B}'(t)&=\left(\sum_{i=0}^n\mathbf{p}_ib_{i,n}(t)\right)'\\ &=\sum_{i=0}^n\mathbf{p}_i\binom{n}{i}\left((1-t)^{n-i}t^i\right)'\\ &=\sum_{i=0}^n\mathbf{p}_i\binom{n}{i}\left(-(n-i)(1-t)^{n-i-1}t^i+i(1-t)^{n-i}t^{i-1}\right)\\ &=n\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}b_{i,n-1}(t)(\mathbf{p}_{i+1}-\mathbf{p}_i) \nonumber \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Similarly, we can obtain the second-order derivative:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mathbf{B}''(t)&=n\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}\left(b_{i,n-1}(t)\right)'(\mathbf{p}_{i+1}-\mathbf{p}_i)\\ &=n\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}(\mathbf{p}_{i+1}-\mathbf{p}_i)\left(-(n-1-i)\binom{n-1}{i}(1-t)^{n-2-i}t^i+i\binom{n-1}{i}(1-t)^{n-1-i}t^{i-1}\right)\\ &=n(n-1)\sum_{i=0}^{n-2}b_{i,n-2}(t)(\mathbf{p}_{i+2}-2\mathbf{p}_{i+1}+\mathbf{p}_i) \nonumber \end{aligned} \end{equation}

We will use these results later soon.

Degree elevation

Suppose B(t)\mathbf{B}(t)B(t) is an nnn-th order Bézier curve and we want to elevate its degree to n+1n+1n+1. We can do this via the following process:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mathbf{B}(t)&=(1-t)\mathbf{B}(t)+t\mathbf{B}(t)\\ &=(1-t)\sum_{i=0}^n\mathbf{p}_ib_{i,n}(t)+t\sum_{i=0}^n\mathbf{p}_ib_{i,n}(t)\\ &=\sum_{i=0}^n\frac{n+1-i}{n+1}b_{i,n+1}(t)\mathbf{p}_i + \sum_{i=0}^n\frac{i+1}{n+1}b_{i+1,n+1}(t)\mathbf{p}_i\\ &=\sum_{i=0}^{n+1}\left(\frac{n+1-i}{n+1}\mathbf{p}_i+\frac{i}{n+1}\mathbf{p}_{i-1}\right)b_{i,n+1}(t)\\ &=\sum_{i=0}^{n+1}\mathbf{p}'_{i}b_{i,n+1}~~\left(\mathbf{p}'_{i}=\frac{n+1-i}{n+1}\mathbf{p}_i+\frac{i}{n+1}\mathbf{p}_{i-1}\right) \nonumber \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Note that when i=0i=0i=0, p0′=p0\mathbf{p}'_0=\mathbf{p}_0p0′​=p0​, and when i=n+1i=n+1i=n+1, pn+1′=pn\mathbf{p}'_{n+1}=\mathbf{p}_npn+1′​=pn​. So p−1\mathbf{p}_{-1}p−1​ and pn+1\mathbf{p}_{n+1}pn+1​ can be arbitrary. We simply set them to zero.

Composite Bézier curves

A composite Bézier curve is a series of Bézier curves joined end to end where the first point of one curve coincides with the last point of the previous curve. If a Bézier curve is composite, we say it’s C0\mathsf{C}^0C0 continuous.

However, sometimes we want higher degrees of continuity than C0\mathsf{C}^0C0 at the joint of Bézier curves (note that individual curves are C∞\mathsf{C}^\inftyC∞ within their own inteval, and the discontinuity only happens at the meetings of consecutive curves).

Given two Bézier curves [p0,p1,p2,p3][\mathbf{p}_0,\mathbf{p}_1,\mathbf{p}_2,\mathbf{p}_3][p0​,p1​,p2​,p3​] and [p3,p4,p5,p6][\mathbf{p}_3,\mathbf{p}_4,\mathbf{p}_5,\mathbf{p}_6][p3​,p4​,p5​,p6​], we can define different degrees of continuity at p3\mathbf{p}_3p3​:

  • C0/G0\mathsf{C}^0/\mathsf{G}^0C0/G0 (positional continuity): consecutive Bézier curves meet at the same point. This holds by definition. In this case, it’s p3\mathbf{p}_3p3​.

  • C1\mathsf{C}^1C1 (velocity continuity): the incoming and outgoing derivatives of p3\mathbf{p}_3p3​ are the same, vouching for a smooth transition from the first curve to the second. To show this, we let B′(1)first=B′(0)second\mathbf{B}'(1)_\text{first}=\mathbf{B}'(0)_\text{second}B′(1)first​=B′(0)second​, and the solution is 3(p3−p2)=3(p4−p3)3(\mathbf{p}_3-\mathbf{p}_2)=3(\mathbf{p}_4-\mathbf{p}_3)3(p3​−p2​)=3(p4​−p3​). This gives us 2p3=p2+p42\mathbf{p}_3=\mathbf{p}_2+\mathbf{p}_42p3​=p2​+p4​. This equation tells us that p2\mathbf{p}_2p2​ and p4\mathbf{p}_4p4​ are not only colinear with respect to p3\mathbf{p}_3p3​, but their vector lengths are also the same. Rewriting this equation to p4=2p3−p2\mathbf{p}_4 = 2\mathbf{p}_3-\mathbf{p}_2p4​=2p3​−p2​ suggests p4\mathbf{p}_4p4​ is fully determined by p2\mathbf{p}_2p2​ and p3\mathbf{p}_3p3​ if we want C1\mathsf{C}^1C1 continuity.

  • G1\mathsf{G}^1G1 (tangent continuity): p2\mathbf{p}_2p2​ and p4\mathbf{p}_4p4​ are colinear but not requiring their vector lengths to be equal. Thus, we say G1\mathsf{G}^1G1 is less strict than C1\mathsf{C}^1C1. If a composite curve is C1\mathsf{C}^1C1, then it must be G1\mathsf{G}^1G1. To guarantee G1\mathsf{G}^1G1, we just add an extra degree of freedom to the vector length: p4=p3+β(p3−p2) (β>0)\mathbf{p}_4=\mathbf{p}_3+\beta(\mathbf{p}_3-\mathbf{p}_2)~(\beta>0)p4​=p3​+β(p3​−p2​) (β>0). G1\mathsf{G}^1G1 means the two curves share a common tangent direction at the join point.

  • C2\mathsf{C}^2C2 (acceleration continuity): following a similar process to C1\mathsf{C}^1C1 continuity, the C2\mathsf{C}^2C2 continuity can be defined by p5=p1+4(p3−p2)\mathbf{p}_5=\mathbf{p}_1+4(\mathbf{p}_3-\mathbf{p}_2)p5​=p1​+4(p3​−p2​). That is to say, p5\mathbf{p}_5p5​ is fully determined by p1,p2\mathbf{p}_1, \mathbf{p}_2p1​,p2​ and p3\mathbf{p}_3p3​. You should be very careful of using C2\mathsf{C}^2C2 continuity: the C2\mathsf{C}^2C2 continuity causes a loss of two control points p4\mathbf{p}_4p4​ and p5\mathbf{p}_5p5​, leaving only one free control point p6\mathbf{p}_6p6​. You perhaps cannot get the curve shape you expect. If you do not want to loss control over these points, you can use uniform B-splines, which will be introduced later.

  • G2\mathsf{G}^2G2 (curvature continuity): this type of continuity requires kB′′(1)first=B′′(0)second (k>0)k\mathbf{B}''(1)_\text{first}=\mathbf{B}''(0)_\text{second}~(k>0)kB′′(1)first​=B′′(0)second​ (k>0) except requiring G1\mathsf{G}^1G1, i.e., satisfying p5=p3+(p3−p2)(2β1+β12+β2/2)+(p1−p2)β12\mathbf{p}_5=\mathbf{p}_3+(\mathbf{p}_3-\mathbf{p}_2)(2\beta_1+\beta_1^2+\beta_2/2)+(\mathbf{p}_1-\mathbf{p}_2)\beta^2_1p5​=p3​+(p3​−p2​)(2β1​+β12​+β2​/2)+(p1​−p2​)β12​. To obtain this result, first expand kB′′(1)first=B′′(0)secondk\mathbf{B}''(1)_\text{first}=\mathbf{B}''(0)_\text{second}kB′′(1)first​=B′′(0)second​ and then plug p4=p3+β(p3−p2)\mathbf{p}_4=\mathbf{p}_3+\beta(\mathbf{p}_3-\mathbf{p}_2)p4​=p3​+β(p3​−p2​) in. Last, substitute for kkk with β12\beta_1^2β12​ and β\betaβ with β1+β2/4\beta_1+\beta_2/4β1​+β2​/4, and you will get the right formula. Curvature continuity implies that the curves share a common center of curvature at the join point.

  • C3\mathsf{C}^3C3 (jolt continuity): if a composite Bézier curve is C3\mathsf{C}^3C3, it satisfies p6=p3+(p3−p0)+6(p1−2p2+p3)\mathbf{p}_6=\mathbf{p}_3+(\mathbf{p}_3-\mathbf{p}_0)+6(\mathbf{p}_1-2\mathbf{p}_2+\mathbf{p}_3)p6​=p3​+(p3​−p0​)+6(p1​−2p2​+p3​). In this case, the fist curve full determines the second curve and thus the third, forth curve, etc. Now we say the generated composite Bézier curve is C∞\mathsf{C}^\inftyC∞ as there is indeed no joins.

If a curve is Cn\mathsf{C}^nCn, then it must be Gn\mathsf{G}^nGn. If a curve is Cn/Gn\mathsf{C}^n/\mathsf{G}^nCn/Gn, then it must be Cn−1/Gn−1\mathsf{C}^{n-1}/\mathsf{G}^{n-1}Cn−1/Gn−1.

Approximating circular arcs

A cubic Bézier curve can be used to approximate a circular arc. Let’s first start with a simple case: the arc starts at point a\mathbf{a}a and ends at points b\mathbf{b}b, placed at equal distances above and below the xxx-axis in the first quadrant, spanning an arc of angle θ=2ϕ\theta=2\phiθ=2ϕ. The radius is RRR.

Assume the additional control points are a′\mathbf{a}'a′ and b′\mathbf{b}'b′, and their indices are respectively (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) and (x,−y)(x, -y)(x,−y). Note that a(x^,y^),b(x^,y^)\mathbf{a}(\hat{x},\hat{y}), \mathbf{b}(\hat{x},\hat{y})a(x^,y^​),b(x^,y^​), or a(Rcos⁡ϕ,Rsin⁡ϕ),b(Rcos⁡ϕ,−Rsin⁡ϕ)\mathbf{a}(R\cos\phi, R\sin\phi), \mathbf{b}(R\cos\phi, -R\sin\phi)a(Rcosϕ,Rsinϕ),b(Rcosϕ,−Rsinϕ).

According to the expanded Bézier curve expression:

B(t)=(1−t)3p0+3(1−t)2tp1+3(1−t)t2p2+t3p3, 0≤t≤1\mathbf{B}(t)=(1-t)^3\mathbf{p}_0+3(1-t)^2t\mathbf{p}_1+3(1-t)t^2\mathbf{p}_2+t^3\mathbf{p}_3,~0\le t\le 1 B(t)=(1−t)3p0​+3(1−t)2tp1​+3(1−t)t2p2​+t3p3​, 0≤t≤1

we have B(1/2)=(R,0)\mathbf{B}(1/2)=(R,0)B(1/2)=(R,0), which gives:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \mathbf{B}(1/2)&=(R,0)\\ \frac{1}{8}\mathbf{a}+\frac{3}{8}\mathbf{a}'+\frac{3}{8}\mathbf{b}'+\frac{1}{8}\mathbf{b}&=(R,0)\\ \left(\frac{1}{4}\hat{x}+\frac{3}{4}x,0\right)&=(R,0)\\ x&=\frac{4R-\hat{x}}{3} \end{aligned} \end{equation}

On the other hand, according to the derivative of a cubic Bézier curve B′(t)=−3(1−t)2a+3(1−t)(1−3t)a′+3t(2−2t)b′+3t2b\mathbf{B}'(t)=-3(1-t)^2\mathbf{a}+3(1-t)(1-3t)\mathbf{a}'+3t(2-2t)\mathbf{b}'+3t^2\mathbf{b}B′(t)=−3(1−t)2a+3(1−t)(1−3t)a′+3t(2−2t)b′+3t2b, we know the derivative at t=0t=0t=0 is 3(a′−a)3(\mathbf{a}'-\mathbf{a})3(a′−a). It is equal to the tangent at a\mathbf{a}a multiplied by some coefficient kkk:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} 3(\mathbf{a}'-\mathbf{a})&=k(-\hat{y},\hat{x})\\ 3(x-\hat{x},y-\hat{y})&=k(-\hat{y},\hat{x})\\ y&=k\frac{\hat{x}}{3}+\hat{y}\\ y&=\hat{y}-\frac{\hat{x}(x-\hat{x})}{\hat{y}}\\ y&=\frac{(R-\hat{x})(3R-\hat{x})}{3\hat{y}} \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Now we’ve obtained the control points a′(x,y)\mathbf{a}'(x,y)a′(x,y) and b′(x,−y)\mathbf{b}'(x,-y)b′(x,−y).

What about a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b are not symmetrical? Assume the angle of a\mathbf{a}a is θ\thetaθ and the angle spanned by the arc is ϕ\phiϕ, where the end point is b\mathbf{b}b. We can first rotate both a\mathbf{a}a and b\mathbf{b}b by −(θ+ϕ2)-\left(\theta+\frac{\phi}{2}\right)−(θ+2ϕ​). This now becomes the case discussed above. We can calculate the index of a(x,y)\mathbf{a}(x,y)a(x,y) after being rotated:

a~=[x~y~]=[cos⁡Φ−sin⁡Φsin⁡Φcos⁡Φ][xy],  Φ←−(θ+ϕ2)\tilde{\mathbf{a}}=\begin{bmatrix}\tilde{x}\\\tilde{y}\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}\cos\Phi & -\sin\Phi\\\sin\Phi & \cos\Phi\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\end{bmatrix},~~\Phi\gets-\left(\theta+\frac{\phi}{2}\right) a~=[x~y~​​]=[cosΦsinΦ​−sinΦcosΦ​][xy​],  Φ←−(θ+2ϕ​)

Now we can follow the aforementioned process to calculate the control points:

a~′=(4R−x~3,(R−x~)(3R−x~)3y~),b~′=(4R−x~3,−(R−x~)(3R−x~)3y~)\tilde{\mathbf{a}}'=\left(\frac{4R-\tilde{x}}{3},\frac{(R-\tilde{x})(3R-\tilde{x})}{3\tilde{y}}\right), \tilde{\mathbf{b}}'=\left(\frac{4R-\tilde{x}}{3},-\frac{(R-\tilde{x})(3R-\tilde{x})}{3\tilde{y}}\right) a~′=(34R−x~​,3y~​(R−x~)(3R−x~)​),b~′=(34R−x~​,−3y~​(R−x~)(3R−x~)​)

Last, we rotate a~′\tilde{\mathbf{a}}'a~′ and b~′\tilde{\mathbf{b}}'b~′ back to the original positions:

a′=[cos⁡Φsin⁡Φ−sin⁡Φcos⁡Φ]a~′,  b′=[cos⁡Φsin⁡Φ−sin⁡Φcos⁡Φ]b~′\mathbf{a}'=\begin{bmatrix}\cos\Phi & \sin\Phi\\-\sin\Phi & \cos\Phi\end{bmatrix}\tilde{\mathbf{a}}',~~\mathbf{b}'=\begin{bmatrix}\cos\Phi & \sin\Phi\\-\sin\Phi & \cos\Phi\end{bmatrix}\tilde{\mathbf{b}}' a′=[cosΦ−sinΦ​sinΦcosΦ​]a~′,  b′=[cosΦ−sinΦ​sinΦcosΦ​]b~′

Cubic Hermite splines

Cubic Hermite splines construct a curve based on two points along with their respecive tangents.

Suppose the given starting point is p0\mathbf{p}_0p0​ at t=0t=0t=0 and ending point p1\mathbf{p}_1p1​ at t=1t=1t=1, as well as the starting tangent d0\mathbf{d}_0d0​ and d1\mathbf{d}_1d1​. The fitted cubic polynomial can be defined by:

p(t)=(2t3−3t2+1)p0+(t3−2t2+t)d0+(−2t3+3t2)p1+(t3−t2)d1\mathbf{p}(t)=(2t^3-3t^2+1)\mathbf{p}_0+(t^3-2t^2+t)\mathbf{d}_0+(-2t^3+3t^2)\mathbf{p}_1+(t^3-t^2)\mathbf{d}_1 p(t)=(2t3−3t2+1)p0​+(t3−2t2+t)d0​+(−2t3+3t2)p1​+(t3−t2)d1​

Proof is quite simple. Assume our function is p(t)=at3+bt2+ct+d\mathbf{p}(t)=at^3+bt^2+ct+dp(t)=at3+bt2+ct+d, and given that p(0)=p0,p(1)=p1,p′(0)=d0,p′(1)=d1\mathbf{p}(0)=\mathbf{p}_0, \mathbf{p}(1)=\mathbf{p}_1, \mathbf{p}'(0)=\mathbf{d}_0, \mathbf{p}'(1)=\mathbf{d}_1p(0)=p0​,p(1)=p1​,p′(0)=d0​,p′(1)=d1​, we have:

{d= p0a+b+c+d= p1c= d03a+2b+c= d1\begin{cases} d&=~\mathbf{p}_0\\ a+b+c+d&=~\mathbf{p}_1\\ c&=~\mathbf{d}_0\\ 3a+2b+c&=~\mathbf{d}_1 \end{cases} ⎩⎪⎪⎪⎪⎨⎪⎪⎪⎪⎧​da+b+c+dc3a+2b+c​= p0​= p1​= d0​= d1​​

Solving it gives us:

p(t)=(2p0−2p1+d0+d1)t3+(−3p0+3p1−2d0−d1)t2+d0t+p0\mathbf{p}(t)=(2\mathbf{p}_0-2\mathbf{p}_1+\mathbf{d}_0+\mathbf{d}_1)t^3+(-3\mathbf{p}_0+3\mathbf{p}_1-2\mathbf{d}_0-\mathbf{d}_1)t^2+\mathbf{d}_0t+\mathbf{p}_0 p(t)=(2p0​−2p1​+d0​+d1​)t3+(−3p0​+3p1​−2d0​−d1​)t2+d0​t+p0​

We can rewrite p(t)\mathbf{p}(t)p(t) as follows:
