The Jittering Issue with Damping in Cinemachine and How to Tackle it

Posted on 2023-07-08 Edited on 2023-07-15 In 游戏 - 相机 Disqus: Word count in article: 35k Reading time ≈ 32 mins.

If you are familiar with Cinemachine. you probably know there is a knotty problem with Cinemachine’ damping if you are using Framing Transposer or some other components to track a follow point. That is, the camera jitters with damping enabled under unstable frame rate. The more unstable frame rate is, the more heavily camera will jitter. This post will discuss this phenomenon and proposes a workaround to solve this issue.

Camera jitters with damping in Cinemachine

Unity’s Cinemachine has a notoriously severe problem that may cause the follow object to seemingly jitter when you are using the Framing Transposer component with damping enabled.

To show this, I did a simple experiment. I created a new blank scene and spawned a new attached with the following script:

This script moves the cube for 5 seconds and then keeps it steady for another 5 seconds and continues moving. The move speed as well as the fps (frames per second) can be set for test under different conditions.

A new virtual camera is then created with a Framing Transposer component following this cube. A default damping of 0.2 is used.

Here is result with speed is 100 and fps is 0 (when set to 0, the real fps is determined by Unity, may but unstable).

The jitters are very clear. You can also notice that the frame rate (presented in the Statistics panel) is very unstable, and we will know soon it is the unstable fps that results in camera jitters.

Cinemachine proposes a workaround to alleviate this problem, that is, to use the revised version of damping where they sub-divide each frame and simulates damping in the consecutive series of sub-frames. To enable this functionality, go to Edit -> Project Settings -> Player -> Script Compilation and add the CINEMACHINE_EXPERIMENTAL_DAMPING marco to it.

OKay, now we have enabled the new damping algorithm and let’s see how it will mitigate the jittering issue. Here is result with the same setting we used in our previous experiment, i.e., speed is 100 and fps is 0.

It is astonishing to see the jittering issue becomes even more severe. I conjecture that the variance of fps will significantly amplify camera jitters when this feature is enabled. In other words, the experimental damping algorithm responds to the variance of fps in a NON-linear way: when the variance is small, the experiment damping will reduce the gaps of camera location between contiguous frames; but when the variance is large, it will enlarge the gaps, leading to unacceptable jittering. (Note: I did not validate this conjecture. If you are interested, just review the code and test it yourself.)

What about the expected result if fps is stable? Let’s take more experiments!

Here is result with speed is 100 and fps is 120 (very high fps, which is usually prohibitive in shipped games).

Very steady camera! What about setting fps to 60? Here is the result.

An fps of 60 performs equally well with 120, which is anticipated as fps is stable. Okay, let’s try a final experiment where fps is set at an extreme value of 20.

Even a low fps of 20 makes our camera stable, only if fps itself is stable.

Now we can conclude that it is the instability of fps that induces camera jitters, regardless of the exact value of fps. But, why?

Why camera jitters

Before answering this question, let us first take a look at the source of damping implemented in Cinemachine.

Translating into mathematics, we have:

ℓDamp=ℓInput⋅(1−exp⁡(ln⁡(0.01)ΔTT))\ell_\text{Damp}=\ell_\text{Input}\cdot\left(1-\exp\left(\ln(0.01)\frac{\Delta T}{T}\right)\right) ℓDamp​=ℓInput​⋅(1−exp(ln(0.01)TΔT​))

where TTT is the damp time parameter and ΔT\Delta TΔT the elapsed time in this frame. This equation decays the input ℓinput\ell_\text{input}ℓinput​, the distance for the camera to go to the desired position, by an exponential factor exp⁡(ln⁡(0.01)ΔTT)\exp\left(\ln(0.01)\frac{\Delta T}{T}\right)exp(ln(0.01)TΔT​). If ΔT=T\Delta T=TΔT=T, the residual will be 0.01ℓinput0.01\ell_\text{input}0.01ℓinput​, meaning that at this frame, the camera will traverse 99% of the desired distance to go, only remaining 1% amount for future frames.

Having all variables fully prepared, we can then simulate the object movement and camera track process.

Without loss of generality, we can set v=1v=1v=1. The following sections will use this settings unless otherwise stated.

For different combinations of ΔT(i)\Delta T^{(i)}ΔT(i), P(k),Q(k),R(k)P^{(k)}, Q^{(k)}, R^{(k)}P(k),Q(k),R(k) may have different results. Let’s dive into ΔT(i)\Delta T^{(i)}ΔT(i) and see how it influences the results.

Case 1: Stable FPS, all ΔT(i)\Delta T^{(i)}ΔT(i) are equal

When all ΔT(i)\Delta T^{(i)}ΔT(i) are equal, say ΔT\Delta TΔT, our equations reduce to:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} P^{((k-1)\to k)}&=\Delta T\\ P^{(k)}&=k\Delta T\\ R^{(k)}&=\Delta T\cdot\frac{\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}}{1-\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}}\cdot\left(1-\left(\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}\right)^{k+1}\right)\\ Q^{(k)}&=\Delta T\cdot\left(1-\left(\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}\right)^{k+1}\right) \end{aligned} \end{equation}

R(k)R^{(k)}R(k) apparently has a limitation of R=ΔT⋅emΔTT/(1−emΔTT)\mathfrak{R}=\Delta T\cdot\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}/(1-\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}})R=ΔT⋅emTΔT​/(1−emTΔT​) when k→∞k\to\inftyk→∞ since m=ln⁡(0.01)<0m=\ln(0.01)<0m=ln(0.01)<0. This explains why a camera with damping always has a maximum distance to its following target. There maximum distance, also the supremum, is exactly R\mathfrak{R}R. When TTT is larger, R\mathfrak{R}R will be larger, implying the maximum distance between the camera and its following target will be larger.

What if ΔT(i)\Delta T^{(i)}ΔT(i) is mutable? In this case, we can assume there exists an upper bound ΔT\Delta TΔT such that all ΔT(i)\Delta T^{(i)}ΔT(i) satisty ∣ΔT(i)∣≤ΔT|\Delta T^{(i)}|\le \Delta T∣ΔT(i)∣≤ΔT. Then we are able to derive the same conclusion.

Another question is, why camera does not jitter when FPS is stable? We turn to examine the sign of R(k)−R(k−1)R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}R(k)−R(k−1):

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}&=\Delta T\cdot\frac{\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}}{1-\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}}\cdot\left(1-\left(\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}\right)^{k+1}\right) - \Delta T\cdot\frac{\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}}{1-\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}}\cdot\left(1-\left(\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}\right)^{k}\right)\\ &=\Delta T\cdot\frac{\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}}{1-\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}}\left(\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}\right)^k\left(1-\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}\right)\\ &=\Delta T\cdot \left(\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T}{T}}\right)^{k+1}\\ &>0 \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Therefore, when FPS is stable, R(k)R^{(k)}R(k) is always larger than R(k−1)R^{(k-1)}R(k−1), and jitter will never happen.

Case 2: Unstable FPS, ΔT(i)\Delta T^{(i)}ΔT(i) vary

When FPS is unstable, where ΔT(i)\Delta T^{(i)}ΔT(i) may mutate, how will the camera move in response to its following target? We can still examine the sign of R(k)−R(k−1)R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}R(k)−R(k−1), but in another way:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}&=(R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)})\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T^{(k)}}{T}}-R^{(k-1)}\\ &=P^{((k-1)\to k)}\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T^{(k)}}{T}} - R^{(k-1)}\left(1-\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T^{(k)}}{T}}\right) \end{aligned} \end{equation}

This equation uncovers why camera jitters happen with unstable FPS. The residual at the k-th frame R(k)R^{(k)}R(k) is essentially an interpolation between the following target’s current position increment P((k−1)→k)P^{((k-1)\to k)}P((k−1)→k) and the last frame’s negative residual −R(k−1)-R^{(k-1)}−R(k−1), where the interpolation strength is the decaying factor emΔT(k)T\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T^{(k)}}{T}}emTΔT(k)​. As both P((k−1)→k)P^{((k-1)\to k)}P((k−1)→k) and −R(k−1)-R^{(k-1)}−R(k−1) are fixed, a change in ΔT(k)\Delta T^{(k)}ΔT(k) will incline the resulting residual R(k)R^{(k)}R(k) to different ends, either P((k−1)→k)P^{((k-1)\to k)}P((k−1)→k) or −R(k−1)-R^{(k-1)}−R(k−1).

In our simplified case in which the target moves at a fixed speed in the direction of x-axis, P((k−1)→k)P^{((k-1)\to k)}P((k−1)→k) will always be positive (though its magnitude can vary) and −R(k−1)-R^{(k-1)}−R(k−1) will always be negative. A mutating ΔT(k)\Delta T^{(k)}ΔT(k) thus has a chance to alter the sign of R(k)−R(k−1)R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}R(k)−R(k−1), which further brings about camera jitters.

So when will camera jitter? From the above equation, we know that camera will jitter when the sign of R(k)−R(k−1)R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}R(k)−R(k−1) consistently changes over time, i.e., the value of R(k)−R(k−1)R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}R(k)−R(k−1) oscillates around zero. Let’s make it equal to zero and see what we can find then.

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}&=0\\ (R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)})\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T^{(k)}}{T}}-R^{(k-1)}&=0\\ \frac{R^{(k-1)}}{R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}}&=\mathrm{e}^{m\frac{\Delta T^{(k)}}{T}}\\ m\frac{\Delta T^{(k)}}{T}&=\ln\left(\frac{R^{(k-1)}}{R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}}\right)\\ \Delta T^{(k)}&=\frac{T}{m}\ln\left(\frac{R^{(k-1)}}{R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}}\right) \end{aligned} \end{equation}

This equation tells us when ΔT(k)\Delta T^{(k)}ΔT(k) is near Tmln⁡(R(k−1)R(k−1)+P((k−1)→k))\frac{T}{m}\ln\left(\frac{R^{(k-1)}}{R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}}\right)mT​ln(R(k−1)+P((k−1)→k)R(k−1)​), camera will have a large chance to jitter. This motivates us to improve damping by filtering out the occasions where ΔT(k)\Delta T^{(k)}ΔT(k) is very close to Tmln⁡(R(k−1)R(k−1)+P((k−1)→k))\frac{T}{m}\ln\left(\frac{R^{(k-1)}}{R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}}\right)mT​ln(R(k−1)+P((k−1)→k)R(k−1)​).

What about going deeper? We can treat ΔT(i)\Delta T^{(i)}ΔT(i) as variable, and all other as constants. This abstraction gives us a function of f(x)f(x)f(x):

f(x)=x−aln⁡(bb+vx), a≤0,b>0,v>0,x>0f(x)=x-a\ln\left(\frac{b}{b+vx}\right),~a\le0, b> 0, v>0, x>0 f(x)=x−aln(b+vxb​), a≤0,b>0,v>0,x>0

Taking the derivative of f(x)f(x)f(x), we know that f(x)f(x)f(x) is monotonically decreasing when x∈(−∞,(−av−b)/v)x\in(-\infty, (-av-b)/v)x∈(−∞,(−av−b)/v) and monotonically increasing when x∈((−av−b)/v,+∞)x\in((-av-b)/v,+\infty)x∈((−av−b)/v,+∞), and f(0)=0f(0)=0f(0)=0. Hence, to make the sign of f(x)f(x)f(x) mutable, (−av−b)/v(-av-b)/v(−av−b)/v must be positive and the minimum of f(x)f(x)f(x) must be negative.

The minimum of f(x)f(x)f(x) can be easily computed:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} f(x)_\text{min}&=f((-av-b)/v)\\ &=-a-\frac{b}{v}-a\ln\left(-\frac{b}{av}\right)\\ &=a\left(-1-\frac{b}{av}-\ln\left(-\frac{b}{av}\right)\right)\\ &\xlongequal{x\triangleq-b/av}a\left(-1+x-\ln(x)\right) ~(x\ge 0)\\ &\le 0 \end{aligned} \end{equation}

This reveals the fact that: when (−av−b)/v>0(-av-b)/v>0(−av−b)/v>0, a variant ΔT(k)\Delta T^{(k)}ΔT(k) is likely to cause R(k)−R(k−1)R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}R(k)−R(k−1) to change its sign, thus resulting in camera jitters. Suppose ΔT(k)\Delta T^{(k)}ΔT(k) is large enough, so then the k-th residual R(k)R^{(k)}R(k) gets smaller than R(k−1)R^{(k-1)}R(k−1) while f(x)(k)f(x)^{(k)}f(x)(k) is positive. A smaller R(k)R^{(k)}R(k) pushes bbb to become smaller for the next frame, which further pushes the root of the function to become larger. In this case, even with the same delta time, f(x)(k)f(x)^{(k)}f(x)(k) will have a larger chance wo fall in the negative area, i.e., ΔT(k+1)\Delta T^{(k+1)}ΔT(k+1) is more likely to be less than the root.

Solutions

Solution 1: imposing an invalid range

Based on what we’ve discussed so far, we can immediately come up with a simple solution: enforce ΔT(k)\Delta T^{(k)}ΔT(k) to be O=Tmln⁡(R(k−1)R(k−1)+P((k−1)→k))\mathfrak{O}=\frac{T}{m}\ln\left(\frac{R^{(k-1)}}{R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}}\right)O=mT​ln(R(k−1)+P((k−1)→k)R(k−1)​) if they are very close. That is to say, we use a small value ε\varepsilonε, if ΔT(k)/O∈(1−ε,1+ε)\Delta T^{(k)}/\mathfrak{O}\in(1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon)ΔT(k)/O∈(1−ε,1+ε), we just set ΔT(k)\Delta T^{(k)}ΔT(k) to O\mathfrak{O}O.

Note that R(k−1)R(k−1)+P((k−1)→k)\frac{R^{(k-1)}}{R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}}R(k−1)+P((k−1)→k)R(k−1)​ can be zero or negative. If this is the case, we keep the original ΔT(k)\Delta T^{(k)}ΔT(k) without doing anything. Besides, you should be aware that ΔT(k)\Delta T^{(k)}ΔT(k) here is not the time this frame actually takes, instead, it is just the duration used to calculate damping.

Let us explain it more quantitatively. Suppose ΔT(k)/O=1−δ\Delta T^{(k)}/\mathfrak{O}=1-\deltaΔT(k)/O=1−δ, where 0<δ<ε0<\delta<\varepsilon0<δ<ε. Then ΔT(k)←O\Delta T^{(k)}\gets\mathfrak{O}ΔT(k)←O according to our algorithm. We then plug ΔTδ(k)=O(1−δ)\Delta T^{(k)}_\delta=\mathfrak{O}(1-\delta)ΔTδ(k)​=O(1−δ) into the original expression of R(k)−R(k−1)R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}R(k)−R(k−1):

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} R^{(k)}_\delta-R^{(k)} &= (R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)})\mathrm{e}^{\frac{m}{T}(1-\delta)\frac{T}{m}\ln\left(\frac{R^{(k-1)}}{R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}}\right)}-R^{(k-1)}\\ &=\left(R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}\right)\cdot\left(\frac{R^{(k-1)}}{R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}}\right)^{1-\delta}-R^{(k-1)}\\ &=\left(R^{(k-1)}\right)^{1-\delta}\cdot \left(R^{(k-1)}+P^{((k-1)\to k)}\right)^{\delta}-R^{(k-1)}\\ &>\left(R^{(k-1)}\right)^{1-\delta}\cdot\left(R^{(k-1)}\right)^{\delta}-R^{(k-1)}\\ &>0 \end{aligned} \end{equation}

This demonstrates that now the camera lags behind its following target more than the previous frame since the residual is larger. After substituting ΔTδ(k)\Delta T^{(k)}_\deltaΔTδ(k)​ with O\mathfrak{O}O, R(k)−R(k−1)R^{(k)}-R^{(k-1)}R(k)−R(k−1) would be zero, meaning that the camera now keeps the same frame as last frame. Camera does not jitter.

Here comes the question: what if the following target slows down, or stops, or even turns back to the opposite direction and the camera still remains the same residual to it?

It is quite a good question. But if we look carefully at the function of f(x)f(x)f(x), we will find this situation will never happen. Let’s rewrite f(x)f(x)f(x) here:

f(x)=x−aln⁡(bb+vx), a≤0,b>0,x>0f(x)=x-a\ln\left(\dfrac{b}{b+vx}\right), ~a\le 0, b>0, x>0 f(x)=x−aln(b+vxb​), a≤0,b>0,x>0

Solution 2: adding low-pass filter

Our improved samping perfectly solves camera jitters under unstable fps, but it looks very stiff when it reaches the boundary of max damping distance. Can we make it more realistic so that the object won’t just look stolid? Yes of course, we can add low-pass filter, or moving average to our improved damping to achieve more smooth results.

Okay, let’s forget all aforementioned solutions and revisit our residual update formula at the very beginning:

And as the velocity vvv may change over time, a closed-form of RtR_tRt​ cannor serve our purpose well. We can only incrementally calculate camera residuals at each frame based on what we currently have.

RtR_tRt​ is a monotonic increasing function, and of course, it’s continuous. The continuity ensures that the camera trajectory is always smooth and never jitters, if fps is sufficiently high (over one thousand I suppose?).
